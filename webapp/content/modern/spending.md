The modern federal budget is, more than anything else, a machine for social insurance and
health financing. The largest categories of spending are Social Security, the major health
programs (Medicare and Medicaid), and—growing quickly as interest rates have risen—the
interest on the national debt. Defense remains the largest single *discretionary* program,
but mandatory social and health spending dwarfs it.

## Spending by function over time

Classifying spending by **function**—what the money is *for*—rather than by agency reveals
the long-run shift toward health and income support. The functional view used here follows
the international COFOG standard, which makes American spending comparable with other
countries.

## Why the composition is shifting

Three forces drive the long-run rise in social and health spending. First,
**demographics**: an aging population means more Social Security and Medicare
beneficiaries relative to workers. Second, **health-cost growth**: medical prices have
historically risen faster than the general price level. Third, **interest costs**: as the
debt has grown and rates have risen, interest has become one of the fastest-growing lines
in the budget—a theme developed in the [Treasury section](/treasury) and the [deficits and
debt](/modern/deficits-debt) page.[cite:cbo_ltbo_2024][cite:worldbank_wdi]
