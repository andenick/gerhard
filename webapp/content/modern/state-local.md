Federal finance is only half the picture. State and local governments together account for
a large share of total government spending in the United States, and they fund the services
Americans encounter most directly: schools, police and fire, roads, water, and much of
public health and welfare administration.

## A different revenue mix

Where the federal government relies on income and payroll taxes, state and local
governments rely on a more varied and more regressive mix: **sales taxes**, **property
taxes**, state income taxes (in most but not all states), and large **transfers from the
federal government**. Because sales and property taxes take a larger share of income from
lower-income households, the state-local system is, on average, less progressive than the
federal system—and varies enormously from state to state.

## The balanced-budget constraint

A defining feature of state and local finance is that, unlike the federal government,
almost all states are required to **balance their operating budgets**. They cannot run
persistent deficits to smooth over recessions, which makes their finances pro-cyclical:
revenue falls in downturns exactly when need rises, forcing spending cuts or tax increases
at the worst moment. Federal transfers—especially the large aid packages of 2009 and
2020–21—exist partly to offset this constraint.

## Cross-state variation

The tax burden Americans face depends heavily on where they live. Some states levy no
income tax at all and rely on sales and property taxes or resource revenue; others maintain
high, progressive income taxes. The project's U.S. state tax estimates capture this
