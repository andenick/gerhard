Federal revenue rests on two pillars: the **individual income tax** and **payroll taxes**
for Social Security and Medicare. Together these supply the large majority of federal
receipts. The corporate income tax, once a far larger share, now contributes a
single-digit percentage, and customs duties and excises—the entire revenue base of the
nineteenth century—are today a rounding error by comparison.

## How much does the federal government collect?

Total federal tax revenue as a share of GDP has fluctuated within a relatively narrow band
for decades, rising in booms and falling in recessions and after tax cuts.

{{chart:us_tax_revenue@1972-2024}}

## How progressive is the system?

The individual income tax is steeply progressive: a large share of it is paid by a small
share of households at the top of the income distribution. The historical record of
income-tax shares by group shows how concentrated income-tax liability has become as top
incomes have risen.

{{chart:us_tax_composition@1980-2024}}

This progressivity is partly offset by the **payroll tax**, which is regressive at the top
because it applies only up to a wage cap and falls only on labor income. The net
effect—combining the progressive income tax, the flatter payroll tax, and the corporate
and excise taxes—is a federal system that is progressive overall, though less so than the
income tax alone suggests.[cite:cbo]

## The distribution of income behind the tax

Any discussion of who *should* pay has to start with who *has* income. The distributional
national accounts show a top 1 percent share that fell sharply during the mid-century
"Great Compression" and has risen substantially since 1980.

{{chart:us_top_income_shares@1960-2024}}

The interaction of a rising concentration of pre-tax income with a progressive rate
structure is what makes the top of the distribution such a large share of income-tax
revenue—and what keeps the question of tax progressivity at the center of American
politics.[cite:piketty_saez_zucman_2018]
