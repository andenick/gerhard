When the federal government spends more than it collects, it runs a **deficit** and covers
the gap by issuing Treasury securities. The accumulation of past deficits, net of
surpluses, is the **debt**. Understanding modern public finance means understanding both
the annual flow and the accumulated stock—and the conditions under which the stock is
sustainable.

## The annual balance

The United States has run deficits in most years since 1970, interrupted only by the brief
surpluses of 1998–2001. Recessions widen deficits automatically—revenue falls and support
spending rises—while the two great crises of the century, 2008 and 2020, produced the
largest peacetime deficits on record.

## The accumulated debt

The stock of debt relative to GDP tells the longer story: a steady decline from the 1946
wartime peak through the mid-1970s, a rise through the 1980s and early 1990s, a dip during
the surplus years, and then a steep climb through the two crises of the twenty-first
century.

## What makes debt sustainable

Debt sustainability is not about a single magic number. The key relationship is between the
**interest rate** the government pays on its debt and the **growth rate** of the economy.
When growth exceeds the effective interest rate, a country can run modest primary deficits
and still see its debt-to-GDP ratio fall—the mechanism that let the postwar United States
grow out of its WWII debt. When interest rates rise above growth, the arithmetic reverses
and debt compounds.

This is why the post-2022 rise in interest rates matters so much, and why the
**structure** of the debt—its average maturity and duration, and how quickly higher rates
pass through to interest costs—has become a central fiscal question. That structure is the
subject of the [Treasury &amp; Duration section](/treasury).[cite:cbo_ltbo_2024]
