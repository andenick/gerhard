The Great Depression and the Second World War remade American public finance on both the
spending and the taxing sides, and left behind a federal government structurally larger
and more central to economic life than anything that had come before.

## The New Deal and the birth of the social-insurance state

Franklin Roosevelt's New Deal created the first durable federal social-spending
programs—most consequentially, the **Social Security Act of 1935**, which established
old-age insurance funded by a dedicated payroll tax. This was a new kind of public
finance: an earmarked tax feeding a social-insurance trust fund, separate from the
general income-tax–funded budget. It is the ancestor of the payroll taxes that today fund
Social Security and Medicare and account for roughly a third of federal revenue.

## The mass income tax

World War II completed the transformation. To finance a total-war economy, the income tax
was converted from a "class tax" paid by the well-off into a **mass tax** paid by the
majority of households. The number of taxpayers rose roughly tenfold, top marginal rates
reached 94 percent, and in 1943 Congress introduced **payroll withholding**, which made
the modern income tax administratively feasible at mass scale.

The distribution of the tax burden shifted accordingly. The very high top rates of the
war years coincided with—and helped produce—a sharp compression of top income shares, the
beginning of what economists later called the "Great Compression."

## Financing the war: debt at its historical peak

The fiscal cost of the war was met overwhelmingly by borrowing. Federal debt rose to a
level never matched before or since: roughly **106 percent of GDP** by 1946. War bonds
were sold to tens of millions of households, and the Federal Reserve held interest rates
low to keep the cost of that debt manageable—an early, formal episode of the Treasury–Fed
coordination that the Treasury section of this site explores in depth.

The combined legacy of this era—a mass income tax, dedicated payroll taxes for social
insurance, large permanent federal programs, and the routine use of debt as a
macroeconomic tool—defined the fiscal system that the postwar United States
inherited.[cite:colm_1955]
