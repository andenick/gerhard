The three decades after 1945 were, in fiscal terms, the most stable in modern American
history. High wartime tax rates were retained in large part, the enormous war debt was
gradually shrunk relative to a fast-growing economy, and a bipartisan consensus accepted a
substantial federal role in the economy.

## Growing out of the debt

The United States did not repay its World War II debt so much as **grow out of it**.
Strong real growth, moderate inflation, and a debt whose interest rate was held below the
growth rate caused the debt-to-GDP ratio to fall steadily from its 1946 peak toward the
mid-1970s, even as the government ran frequent modest deficits. This is the arithmetic of
debt sustainability—when an economy grows faster than the effective interest rate on its
debt, the burden shrinks on its own—and it is central to the modern debate covered in the
Treasury section.

## Top rates stayed high; the compression held

Top marginal income-tax rates remained at or above 70 percent for the entire period, and
the compressed distribution of income that emerged from the war persisted. The top 1
percent's share of national income stayed near its historical lows throughout these
decades—the long plateau of the "Great Compression."

## The Great Society and the rise of social spending

The other great development of the era was the expansion of the social-insurance state.
Lyndon Johnson's Great Society created **Medicare and Medicaid in 1965**, adding federal
health financing to the old-age insurance built in the 1930s. Combined with the
maturation of Social Security, this drove a long-run rise in social and health spending as
a share of the federal budget that continues to this day.

## The end of the consensus

By the 1970s the postwar settlement was under strain. Stagflation—the simultaneous
appearance of high inflation and high unemployment—discredited the prevailing
macroeconomic framework. Inflation pushed households into ever-higher tax brackets without
any vote of Congress ("bracket creep"), generating a tax revolt that began in the states
(California's Proposition 13 in 1978) and reshaped national politics. The stage was set
for the supply-side turn of the 1980s.
