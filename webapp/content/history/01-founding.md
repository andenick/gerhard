When the first Congress met in 1789, the United States was a debtor nation with no
reliable way to raise money. The Articles of Confederation had left the federal
government dependent on requisitions the states routinely ignored. The Constitution's
grant to Congress of the power "to lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises"
was, in large part, a fiscal rescue.

## Hamilton's bargain

Alexander Hamilton's *First Report on the Public Credit* (1790) proposed that the new
federal government assume the Revolutionary War debts of the states and fund them at
par.[cite:hamilton_1790] The political logic was as important as the financial: by
making creditors dependent on the federal government's solvency, Hamilton bound the
moneyed classes to the survival of the Union. The debt, he argued, could be "a national
blessing" if it was funded and serviced reliably.

The revenue to service that debt came almost entirely from a single source: **customs
duties** on imported goods. For most of the period from 1789 to the Civil War, tariffs
supplied roughly 90 percent of federal revenue, supplemented by the sale of public land.
There was no income tax, no payroll tax, and—outside brief wartime experiments—no
internal excise regime of any size.

## A small, externally financed state

This made the antebellum federal government structurally small and its revenue highly
sensitive to trade. Recessions that cut imports cut revenue immediately; the federal
fisc rose and fell with the commercial cycle. The tariff also became the central
political battleground of the era—protective duties favored Northern manufacturing,
while the agricultural, export-oriented South wanted them low. The 1832 Nullification
Crisis over the "Tariff of Abominations" was, at bottom, a fight about public finance.

By 1835 the federal government had done something no modern state has matched: under
President Andrew Jackson it **extinguished the national debt entirely**, even
distributing a surplus to the states. It would not last. The Panic of 1837 and the
expenses of westward expansion returned the government to borrowing.

The antebellum fiscal system was simple, narrow, and external—funded by taxing the
nation's commerce with the world rather than its citizens' incomes. The Civil War would
shatter that model permanently.
