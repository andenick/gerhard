The Civil War transformed American public finance more than any event before the New
Deal. The Union could not fight a continental war on customs duties alone. In 1861, and
more durably in the Revenue Act of 1862, Congress enacted the first federal **income
tax**, created the office of the Commissioner of Internal Revenue, and built a sprawling
system of excises on liquor, tobacco, manufactures, and licenses.

## The fiscal machinery of war

Three instruments financed the Union war effort: vastly expanded taxation, the issuance
of paper money (the "greenbacks"), and unprecedented borrowing. Federal debt exploded
from a negligible sum in 1860 to roughly \$2.7 billion by 1865. Jay Cooke's
mass-marketing of war bonds to ordinary citizens prefigured the Liberty Bond drives of
the next century and broadened the base of public creditors well beyond the financial
elite.

## Repeal, and the long tariff interlude

The income tax was always understood as a war measure. It was allowed to lapse in 1872,
and when Congress revived a peacetime income tax in 1894 the Supreme Court struck it
down in *Pollock v. Farmers' Loan & Trust* (1895), holding that a tax on income from
property was a "direct tax" requiring apportionment among the states by population—an
impossible standard. For the rest of the period, the federal government reverted to its
older sources: high protective tariffs and excise taxes, especially on alcohol and
tobacco, which together funded the Gilded Age state.

## Pressure builds

But the economy the tariff taxed was changing. Industrial fortunes of a scale the
republic had never seen accumulated alongside deep agrarian and urban distress.
Populists and Progressives increasingly attacked the tariff as a regressive tax that
fell on consumers while shielding trusts. The demand for a tax that reached the great new
incomes and fortunes—rather than the grocery bill—became one of the defining reform
causes of the era.

That pressure culminated in the campaign for a constitutional amendment to place the
income tax beyond the reach of *Pollock*. Ratified in 1913, the Sixteenth Amendment
opened the modern era of American public finance—the subject of the next chapter, and
the point at which this project's continuous data series begin.
