The twenty-first century has been an era of crisis finance. Two of the largest fiscal
shocks since World War II—the 2008 global financial crisis and the 2020 pandemic—came
barely a decade apart, and between and after them the United States has run large,
persistent deficits and watched its debt climb back toward the wartime peak.

## Two shocks and a rising debt

The 2008 crisis brought bank rescues, a major stimulus, and a collapse in tax revenue as
the economy contracted. The 2020 pandemic brought the largest peacetime fiscal expansion
in American history—trillions in relief over a matter of months. Each episode pushed
federal debt sharply higher as a share of GDP; together they have brought the publicly
held debt to levels approaching, and on some measures exceeding, the 106 percent of GDP
reached in 1946.

## Where the money goes

The modern federal budget is, increasingly, a social-insurance and health-financing
operation with a military and an interest bill attached. Social Security, Medicare,
Medicaid, and other health and income-support programs dominate spending, and their share
has risen as the population ages.

## The Treasury market moves to the center

What is genuinely new about the current era is the centrality of the **Treasury market**
and the **Federal Reserve's balance sheet** to public finance. Through successive rounds
of quantitative easing after 2008 and again in 2020, the Fed bought trillions of dollars
of Treasury securities, absorbing a large share of the duration risk in the market and
blurring the old line between monetary and fiscal policy. As interest rates rose after
2022, the government's interest costs climbed steeply, making the structure of the
debt—its maturity, its duration, who holds it—a first-order fiscal question.
