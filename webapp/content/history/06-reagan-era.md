The election of 1980 marked a decisive break with the postwar tax consensus. The Economic
Recovery Tax Act of 1981 cut the top marginal income-tax rate from 70 percent toward 50,
and the Tax Reform Act of 1986 cut it further to 28 while broadening the base and
eliminating many shelters. Marginal rates that had stood above 70 percent for four decades
fell to their lowest level since before the New Deal.

## The return of top income concentration

The distributional consequences are among the clearest patterns in the entire record.
After half a century of compression, the top 1 percent's share of national income began a
sustained climb in the 1980s that has continued, with cyclical interruptions, ever since.
The era of high marginal rates and compressed incomes gave way to lower rates and rising
concentration.[cite:piketty_saez_zucman_2018]

## Deficits become structural

The combination of large tax cuts, a defense buildup, and a deep recession in the early
1980s produced the largest peacetime deficits in American history to that point, and the
debt-to-GDP ratio—which had fallen for thirty-five years—began to rise again. Deficit
reduction became the dominant fiscal preoccupation of Washington for two decades,
producing major budget agreements in 1990 (which broke President George H. W. Bush's "no
new taxes" pledge) and 1993.

## The surplus interlude

The story took an unexpected turn at the end of the century. The 1990 and 1993 deficit
deals, spending restraint, the end of the Cold War "peace dividend," and above all the
soaring tax revenues of the late-1990s technology boom combined to produce something not
seen in a generation: **federal budget surpluses** from 1998 through 2001. For a brief
moment, official projections showed the entire publicly held national debt being paid off
within a decade.

It did not happen. The recession of 2001, the tax cuts of 2001 and 2003, and the costs of
the wars that followed September 11 returned the federal government to deficits—and, soon
after, to the largest fiscal shocks since World War II.
