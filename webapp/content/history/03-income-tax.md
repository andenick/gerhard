The ratification of the Sixteenth Amendment in 1913 removed the constitutional obstacle
that had killed the 1894 income tax, and Congress moved immediately to use its new power.
The Revenue Act of 1913 introduced a modern, graduated income tax—at first touching only
the wealthiest households, with a top rate of just 7 percent.

## The income tax becomes a fiscal engine

What began as a modest levy on the rich became, within four years, the financial backbone
of a world war. To fund American entry into World War I, Congress raised the top marginal
rate to 77 percent by 1918 and sharply expanded the reach of the tax. For the first time,
a meaningful share of federal revenue came from taxing income rather than consumption at
the border.

The concentration of income that the new tax was designed to reach is visible in the
distributional record that begins in this period. The top 1 percent's share of national
income stood at extraordinary heights through the 1910s and 1920s.

## The 1920s reversal

The 1920s brought a sharp reversal of wartime tax policy. Treasury Secretary Andrew
Mellon argued that very high marginal rates were self-defeating—that lower rates would
expand the base and the economy. Under three Republican administrations the top rate was
cut repeatedly, reaching 25 percent by 1925. The decade combined falling tax rates at the
top with a booming, increasingly financialized economy and a rising concentration of
income and wealth—a combination that would end abruptly in October
1929.[cite:piketty_saez_zucman_2018]

By the eve of the Depression, the architecture of modern federal finance was in place—a
graduated income tax capable of raising large sums—but the political consensus about how
progressive it should be was anything but settled. The Depression and the New Deal would
answer that question for a generation.
