The Treasury market is often described as risk-free, but its *plumbing*—the rules,
intermediaries, and infrastructure that let trillions of dollars change hands—has been
reshaped repeatedly by regulation and by episodes of stress.

## Why market structure matters

The Treasury market's depth and liquidity are what make Treasuries the world's premier safe
asset and what keep the government's borrowing costs low. But that liquidity is provided by
intermediaries—primary dealers and, increasingly, principal trading firms—whose capacity
is shaped by regulation. Post-2008 bank capital rules, for example, made it more costly for
dealers to hold large Treasury inventories, which some analysts argue has reduced the
market's resilience even as the stock of debt has grown.

## Episodes of stress

Several episodes have tested the market's structure: the 2014 "flash rally," the September
2019 repo-market spike, and above all the March 2020 "dash for cash," when even the
Treasury market briefly seized up and the Federal Reserve intervened on a massive scale to
restore function. Each episode prompted regulatory review and reform proposals, from
central clearing of Treasury trades to changes in how liquidity is provided.
