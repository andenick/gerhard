The most consequential development in the Treasury market of the past two decades was the
Federal Reserve's entry into it at massive scale. Through successive rounds of
**quantitative easing (QE)**—after the 2008 crisis and again in 2020—the Fed bought
trillions of dollars of Treasury securities, fundamentally altering who bears the
interest-rate risk embedded in the national debt.

## What "duration extraction" means

When the Fed buys long-dated Treasuries and holds them on its balance sheet, it removes
that **duration risk** from private markets. Private investors who would otherwise hold
long bonds—and demand compensation for the risk that rates rise—instead hold reserves,
which carry no duration risk. By absorbing duration, the central bank pushes down the
**term premium** and flattens the yield curve, which is precisely the stimulus QE is
designed to deliver.

The scale of this duration extraction is one of the defining features of post-2008 public
finance.

## The fiscal consequences

QE blurred the old line between monetary and fiscal policy. Because the Fed remits its
profits to the Treasury, the government effectively converted a large slice of its
long-term, fixed-rate debt into overnight, floating-rate financing (the interest the Fed
pays on reserves). While rates were near zero this lowered the consolidated government's
interest costs. But it also meant that when the Fed raised rates after 2022, the
consolidated interest bill rose faster than the Treasury's own coupon schedule alone would
suggest—and the Fed's remittances to the Treasury fell, even turning negative.

## The reversal

As the Fed has allowed its balance sheet to shrink ("quantitative tightening"), duration
risk has flowed back to private markets, and the term premium has come under upward
pressure. The dynamics of duration extraction and its reversal—traced in the data on this
page—are now central to any honest accounting of the government's true interest-rate
exposure.[cite:fed_soma][cite:treasury_mspd]
