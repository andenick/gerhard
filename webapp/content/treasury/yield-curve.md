Two mechanisms set the cost of government borrowing: the **yield curve**, which prices
Treasury debt across maturities, and the **auction** process, through which new debt is
actually sold.

## The yield curve

The yield curve plots the interest rate the government pays at each maturity, from
four-week bills to thirty-year bonds. Its **shape** carries economic information. A normal,
upward-sloping curve reflects a positive term premium and expectations of steady growth. A
**flat** or **inverted** curve—short rates above long rates—has historically been one of
the most reliable predictors of recession, and reflects market expectations that the
central bank will eventually cut rates.

The yield curve also interacts directly with Treasury's issuance strategy: the steeper the
curve, the greater the apparent saving from short-term issuance, and the greater the
temptation to shorten the debt's maturity at the cost of higher rollover risk.

## Auctions

The Treasury sells new securities through regular **auctions** open to primary dealers and
other bidders. Two statistics from each auction reveal the health of demand. The
**bid-to-cover ratio** measures how many dollars were bid for each dollar sold—higher is
stronger. The **tail**—the gap between the highest yield the Treasury expected and the
yield it had to accept—measures whether demand fell short; a large tail signals weak
appetite and can move markets.

Auction results are watched closely precisely because the Treasury must roll over and
expand an enormous stock of debt continuously; any sustained weakening of demand would
raise borrowing costs across the entire budget. The auction data series
(`S028`) is available in the [data catalog](/data).[cite:treasury_mspd]
