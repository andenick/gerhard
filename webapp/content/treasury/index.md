When the federal government runs a deficit, it borrows by selling **Treasury
securities**—bills, notes, bonds, and inflation-protected securities—into the deepest and
most important financial market in the world. The structure of that debt is not a
technical footnote. Its maturity, its duration, and who holds it determine how quickly
rising interest rates flow through to the government's interest bill, and how much
interest-rate risk the public sector imposes on—or absorbs from—private markets.

This section uses detailed **Monthly Statement of the Public Debt (MSPD)** data to trace
how the structure of the national debt has evolved.[cite:treasury_mspd]

## Duration: the central concept

**Duration** measures how sensitive the value of a bond—or a whole portfolio of debt—is
to changes in interest rates, and how long, on average, the government has locked in its
borrowing cost. A portfolio with longer duration is insulated from rising rates for longer
but costs more to carry when the curve is upward-sloping; a shorter-duration portfolio is
cheaper day-to-day but reprices quickly when rates move.
