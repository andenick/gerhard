The "national debt" is not one thing. The Monthly Statement of the Public Debt divides it
into two broad categories with very different economic meaning.

## Marketable vs non-marketable

**Marketable debt**—bills, notes, bonds, FRNs, and TIPS—is bought and sold in the open
market and is what most people mean by "the Treasury market." **Non-marketable debt**
includes savings bonds and, crucially, the special securities held in government trust
funds such as Social Security. The latter represents money the government owes itself; it
matters for accounting and for the trust funds' solvency, but it does not trade and does
not impose market-based interest-rate risk.

When analysts discuss duration, the yield curve, or the market impact of issuance, they
are almost always talking about the **marketable** debt held by the public.

## Who holds the debt

Marketable Treasuries are held by a wide range of owners: domestic private investors
(banks, pension funds, mutual funds, households), **foreign** official and private holders,
and the **Federal Reserve**. The holder mix has shifted dramatically over the past two
decades—foreign holdings rose sharply in the 2000s, and the Fed's share expanded
enormously through the quantitative-easing programs examined on the [QE
page](/treasury/qe).

The portfolio's average duration differs by holder, which is why the data tracks duration
separately for the market, foreign holders, and the Fed.

## By security type

Within marketable debt, the mix of bills (one year or less), notes (two to ten years), and
bonds (twenty to thirty years) determines the overall maturity profile. Treasury's
**issuance strategy**—how much to issue at each maturity—is a deliberate policy choice that
trades off the lower average cost of short-term borrowing against the rollover and
interest-rate risk it creates. The detailed security-level data behind these analyses,
including the full MSPD detail tables, is available in the [data catalog](/data).
